CAPUT IV. De ordine disciplinarum.CHAPTER IV. On the order of disciplines.
PRÆCEPTA.PRECEPTS.
Technologia specialis explicat de numero disciplinarum.Special technology explains the number of disciplines.
Numerus ille consideratur ratione fundamenti, & differentiæ.That number is considered in terms of foundation and difference.
Fundamentum est in summis generibus τὸ ὄν, id est, illius, quod mente nostrâ comprehendi potest; itemq; à summis generibus imperfectionis humanæ.The foundation is in the highest categories "the being," that is, of that which can be comprehended by our mind; and also from the highest categories of human imperfection.
Differentia huius numeri est eiusmodi:The difference of this number is of this kind:
Disciplina est homogenea, vel heterogenea.Discipline is homogeneous or heterogeneous.
Disciplina homogenea est unius obiecti, sive materiæ.Homogeneous discipline is of one object, or matter.
Estque totalis, vel partialis.And it is total or partial.
Disciplina totalis, seu propriè dicta, est integrum systema præceptorum homogenecorum.Total discipline, or properly speaking, is an entire system of homogeneous precepts.
Estque liberalis, vel illiberalis.And it is liberal or illiberal.
Disciplina liberalis est, quæ hominem verè doctum & literatum reddit.Liberal discipline is that which makes a man truly learned and literate.
Estque inferior, vel superior.And it is lower or higher.
Disciplina inferior est, quæ inseruit superiori.Lower discipline is that which serves the higher.
Estque philologica, vel philosophica.And it is philological or philosophical.
Disciplina philologica est systema tradens instrumenta philosophiæ, theologiæ, iurisprudentiæ & medicinæ.Philological discipline is a system delivering the instruments of philosophy, theology, jurisprudence, and medicine.
Estque pura, vel media.And it is pure or intermediate.
Disciplina philologica pura est, quæ vel orationem, vel rationem tantùm dirigit. Ibi sunt Lexica, Grammatica, & Rhetorica: hîc est Logica.Pure philological discipline is that which directs speech or reason only. There are Lexicons, Grammar, and Rhetoric: here is Logic.
Disciplina philologica media, sive mixta, est, quæ orationem & rationem simul dirigit: vt Oratoria, & Poetica.The intermediate or mixed philological discipline is that which directs speech and reason simultaneously: as Oratory and Poetics.
Disciplina philosophica est systema tradens res eiusmodi, quarum cognitio reddit hominem philosophum.Philosophical discipline is a system conveying things of such a kind, whose knowledge renders a man a philosopher.
Estque theoretica, vel practica.And it is theoretical or practical.
Disciplina philosophica theoretica est, quæ monstrat naturalem cognitionem veri.The theoretical philosophical discipline is that which demonstrates the natural knowledge of truth.
Estque generalis, vel specialis. Illa est Metaphysica: hæc est substantiæ, vt Pneumatica, & Physica; vel accidentis, vt Mathematica.And it is general or special. The former is Metaphysics; the latter is of substance, like Pneumatics and Physics, or of accident, like Mathematics.
Mathematica est pura, vel media. Illa tantùm est mathematica, vt Arithmetica, & Geometria: hæc est partim mathematica, partim physica; estque alia quantitatis & corporis, vt Cosmographia, Uranometria, & Geographia: alia qualitatis & corporis, vt Optica, & Musica.Mathematics is pure or intermediate. The former is purely mathematics, like Arithmetic and Geometry; the latter is partly mathematics, partly physics; and it is either concerned with quantity and body, like Cosmography, Uranometry, and Geography, or with quality and body, like Optics and Music.
Disciplina philosophica practica est, quæ monstrat actionem boni moralis.The practical philosophical discipline is that which demonstrates the action of moral good.
Estque generalis, vt Ethica: vel specialis, vt Oeconomica, Politica, & Scholastica.And it is general, like Ethics, or special, like Economics, Politics, and Scholastics.
Disciplina superior est, quæ principatù sibi vendicat.Higher discipline is that which claims supremacy for itself.
Estque diuina, vt Theologiæ; vel humana, vt Iurisprudentia, & Medicina.And it is divine, like Theology, or human, like Jurisprudence and Medicine.
Disciplina illiberalis est, quæ hominem verè literatum non reddit.An illiberal discipline is that which does not make a man truly literate.
Disciplina partialis, seu impropriè dicta, est integri alicuius systematis pars: vt Anthropologia est pars Physices.Partial or improperly so-called discipline is a part of some entire system, like Anthropology is a part of Physics.
Disciplina heterogenea est, quæ compilat sua præcepta ex variis disciplinis homogeneis.Heterogeneous discipline is that which compiles its precepts from various homogeneous disciplines.
Estque propriè, vel impropriè dicta. Illa est Mnemonica, Historica, Chronologia, & Architectonica: hîc est Paradoxologia, Gnomologia, &c.And it is properly or improperly so-called. The former includes Mnemonics, History, Chronology, and Architecture; the latter includes Paradoxology, Gnomology, etc.
REGVLÆ.RULES.
I. Disciplinarum numerus est definitus.I. The number of disciplines is defined.
Quia scibilia sunt finita; itemque modus sciendi, necessè quoque est, ut sit definitus nomine scientiarum, sive disciplinarum. Quod autem artes mechanicæ dicuntur infinitæ, id non ἀπλῶς, sed σχεδὸν est accipiendum. Idem iudicium est de infinitis farraginibus, seu Quodlibetis disciplinarum.Because things knowable are finite, and likewise the method of knowing, it is necessary, therefore, that it be defined in terms of the names of sciences or disciplines. As for the fact that mechanical arts are said to be infinite, this must be understood not strictly but rather approximately. The same judgment applies to infinite mixtures or the "Quodlibets" of disciplines.
II. Unus idemque homo potest se occupare cognitione variarum disciplinarum.II. One and the same person can engage in the study of various disciplines.
Primo enim sunt ingenia quædam subtilia, ignea, heroica, &c. quæ sine ullo negotio varias disciplinas addiscunt: id quod vulgaribus, terreis, tardis, &c. ingeniis non est datum. Deinde vocatio, seu officium; sæpenumero requirit cognitionem & usum plurium, vel pauciorum disciplinarum.Firstly, there are certain subtle, fiery, heroic, etc., minds that, without any trouble, learn various disciplines; this is not given to ordinary, earthy, slow, etc., minds. Furthermore, vocation or duty often requires the knowledge and use of more or fewer disciplines.
III. Numerus disciplinarum debet esse adæquatus rebus discendis; itemque sanandæ imperfectioni hominis.III. The number of disciplines ought to be proportionate to the things to be learned, and likewise to the remedying of human imperfection.
Disciplinæ nomen habent a discendo. Itaque numerus ipsarum debet constitui ex varietate rerum discendarum. Qua quidem in re multum est situm. Quemadmodum enim in republica bene constituta multum refert, ut limites agrorum, totiusque provinciæ, sive territorii, recte constituantur; ita interest reipublicæ literariæ, ut disciplinæ, illarumque fines rite herciscantur, ut ita dicam. Hic autem prima lex est, ut numerus disciplinarum neque sit innumerus, neque concisus & iusto brevior, sed adæquet, atque exhauriat ipsas res scibiles, juxta regulam: Scientia secantur, ut res ipsæ: κατὰ μέρος πράγματα,ἄπασα δὲ περὶ τῶν πρώτων λόγος. Secunda lex est, ne res multiplicentur secundum verba. Sæpenumero enim usu venit, ut una eademque res, atque adeo etiam disciplina, duobus vel pluribus insigniatur nominibus, e.g. Metaphysica, Prima philosophia: Physica, Physiologia: Optica, Perspectiva: Politica, Philosophia moralis &c. Tertia lex est, ne partes disciplinarum habeantur pro peculiaribus, & proprie dictis disciplinis, e.g. Stoicheiologia est pars Physicæ; Aretologia, pars Ethicæ. Quarta lex est, ut farragines disciplinarum non confundantur cum ipsis disciplinis. Cæterum summa genera τῶν ὄντων sunt tria: videlicet, θεωρητῇ πρακτική, & ποιητική, id est, entia cadentia sub contemplationem, actionem, & affectionem. Rursus τὰ θεωρητὰ & πρακτὰ sunt naturalia, vel supernaturalia: τὰ ποιητικὰ sunt liberalia, vel illiberalia. Et hæc omnia vel singula, vel conjuncta tractantur. Hinc itaque summa genera disciplinarum sumuntur. Deinde numerus disciplinarum ita debet subduci, ut habeatur ratio imperfectionis hominis. Sunt enim inventæ disciplinæ, ut sanent morbos mentis & corporis; sive, ut ipsum ædificent. Quot itaque modis homo est sanabilis, ut ita dicam, seu ædificabilis, tot debent esse genera disciplinarum. Laborat autem homo triplici morbo, videl. ignorantia mentis, malitiâ voluntatis, & imperitiâ sive inertìâ mentis, linguæ, manus &c. Illam expellit σωφία; istam φρόνησις; hanc, ἀνδρεία μὲν ἐμπλουτίζει. Omnes itaque disciplinæ ad ista tria summa genera possunt reduci.Disciplines derive their name from learning. Therefore, their number should be constituted from the variety of things to be learned. This matter is indeed of great importance. Just as, in a well-ordered republic, it matters greatly that the boundaries of fields, or of the whole province or territory, be correctly established; so it is in the interest of the literary republic that disciplines and their boundaries be properly partitioned, so to speak. Here, the first rule is that the number of disciplines should neither be innumerable nor overly fragmented and too brief, but should match and exhaust the very knowable things, according to the rule: Sciences are divided as things themselves: "According to parts, in all things concerning first principles." The second rule is that things should not be multiplied according to words. Often, in practice, it happens that one and the same thing, and even discipline, is designated by two or more names, e.g., Metaphysics and First Philosophy: Physics and Physiology: Optics and Perspective: Politics and Moral Philosophy, etc. The third rule is that parts of disciplines should not be considered as separate and properly defined disciplines, e.g., Stoicheiology is a part of Physics; Aretology, a part of Ethics. The fourth rule is that compilations of disciplines should not be confused with the disciplines themselves. Moreover, the highest categories of "beings" are three: namely, theoretical, practical, and poetical, that is, entities falling under contemplation, action, and affection. Furthermore, theoretical and practical matters are natural or supernatural; poetical matters are liberal or illiberal. And all these are treated either individually or collectively. Thus, the highest categories of disciplines are derived. Moreover, the number of disciplines should be drawn in such a way that it takes into account human imperfection. For disciplines have been invented to heal the diseases of mind and body, or to edify them. Therefore, as many ways as man is to be healed, so to speak, or edified, there should be as many kinds of disciplines. However, man suffers from threefold ailments, namely, ignorance of the mind, malice of the will, and inexperience or sluggishness of mind, tongue, hand, etc. The first is expelled by wisdom, the second by prudence, and courage enriches the third. Thus, all disciplines can be reduced to these three highest categories.
IV. Philologia & philosophia duobus modis possunt definiri.IV. Philology and philosophy can be defined in two ways.
Primò considerantur absolutè, in sese; atque hâc ratione Philologia est systema artium liberalium, quæ certis instrumentis excolunt orationem & rationem: Philosophia est systema scientiarum & prudentiarum; quas lumine naturæ homo potest assequi. Deinde considerantur relatè, quatenus inseruiunt tribus superioribus facultatibus; atque hoc modo Philologia est systema instrumentorum liberalium, quibus utuntur tres superiores facultates: Philosophia est systema scientiarum & prudentiarum, quæ præparant animum ad tres superiores facultates.Firstly, they are considered absolutely, in themselves; in this sense, Philology is a system of liberal arts, which cultivates speech and reason with certain instruments: Philosophy is a system of sciences and wisdoms, which a man can attain through the light of nature. Then they are considered relatively, as they serve the three higher faculties; in this way, Philology is a system of liberal instruments used by the three higher faculties: Philosophy is a system of sciences and wisdoms that prepare the mind for the three higher faculties.
V. Philologia nunc opponitur philosophiæ, nunc est ipsius pars.V. Philology is sometimes opposed to philosophy, sometimes it is a part of it.
Hoc est, quod Scholasticis terminis ita enuntiatur: Philologia & philosophia sumuntur oppositè, vel compositè. Oppositè, quatenus ad tria ista summa genera & quasi capita, Philologiam, Philosophiam, & Theologiam, omnes disciplinæ liberales referuntur. Compositè; quatenus philosophia dividitur in theoricam, practicam, & poëticam, sive organicam. Ita enim Philologia est pars philosophiæ; quia nempe philosophia poëtica est ipsissima Philologia.This is expressed in Scholastic terms as follows: Philology and philosophy are taken oppositely or compositely. Oppositely, in reference to those three highest categories and, so to speak, heads, all liberal disciplines are related to Philology, Philosophy, and Theology. Compositely; as philosophy is divided into theoretical, practical, and poetic or organic. Thus, Philology is a part of philosophy; because poetic philosophy is precisely Philology itself.
VI. Philologiæ vox sumitur latè, vel strictè.VI. The term Philology is taken broadly or strictly.
Philologia latè sumta est studium, seu amor literarum. Strictè est vel peritia antiquitatis; vel studium seu amor loquendi, aut disserendi. Priori significatu Philologia complectitur cognitionem linguarum, & disciplinarum quæ circa τὸ λόγον versantur, & denique notitiam antiquitatis. Est, inquam, systema omnium artium, quæ viam muniunt ad arcem Philosophiæ. Sic accipitur apud Martianum Capellam lib. 1. de nuptiis Philologiæ, ubi fingit Philologiam nubere Mercurio. Eodem significatu Atheius celeberrimus Grammaticus dictus est Philologus, Suetonio teste in lib. de viris illustrib. Ita Plutarchus in Lucullo ait, Lucullum solitum φιλολόγων τοῖς φιλολόγοις. Et in vitâ Ciceronis, & apud Athenæum lib. 5. eadem est notio huius vocis. Deinde Philologia strictè est idem, quod inuestigatio antiquitatis. Ita sumitur apud Senecam lib. de morte Claudii; ep. epistol. 107. item apud Photium in Bibliotheca. Ita ergo Philologi appellantur, qui hodie dicuntur Antiquarij. Denique iterum strictè sumitur hæc vox, pro comprehensione artium liberalium, quæ versantur circa λόγον ἀρετὴν καὶ ἡθολογίαν. Quæ notio in hâc Encyclopædiâ attenditur.Philology, taken broadly, is the study or love of letters. Strictly, it is either the expertise of antiquity or the study or love of speaking or discussing. In the former sense, Philology encompasses the knowledge of languages and disciplines that revolve around discourse, and, finally, the knowledge of antiquity. It is, I say, a system of all the arts that pave the way to the stronghold of Philosophy. Thus, it is understood in Martianus Capella's Book 1 on the marriage of Philology, where he imagines Philology marrying Mercury. In the same sense, the renowned grammarian Ateius is called Philologist, as noted by Suetonius in the book on illustrious men. Thus, Plutarch in Lucullus says Lucullus was accustomed to "loving words with those who love words." And in the life of Cicero and in Athenaeus Book 5, the notion of this term is the same. Then, Philology, strictly, is the same as the investigation of antiquity. It is taken in this way by Seneca in the book on the death of Claudius; in letter 107, likewise by Photius in the Library. Thus, those who today are called antiquarians are called Philologists. Finally, this term, again strictly, is taken to mean the comprehension of liberal arts, which revolve around discourse, virtue, and ethics. This notion is the one considered in this Encyclopedia.
VII. Philosophiae vocabulum sumitur generaliter, & specialiter.VII. The Term Philosophy Is Taken Generally and Specifically.
In omni legitimâ tractatione fores pandit explicatio vocis, & nominatim liberatio ipsius ab ambiguitate. Tollamus itaque ambiguitatem huius vocis, quæ est φιλοσοφία et τῶν πολλαχῶς λεγομένων. Principio autem philosophiæ vocabulum sumitur generaliter, & quidem vel latissimè, vel minùs late. Latissimè pro omni eruditione & doctrinâ, quæ in animum hominis cadere potest. Et hoc modo sub se complectitur omnes facultates, uti vocant, & ex Cicerone 2. offc. sic finitur: Philosophia est diuinarum humanarumque rerum scientia, earumque causarum, quibus hæres continentur. Confer eundem 3. de oratore, ep. 1. de legibus. Sub hâc latissimâ significatione continentur artes ipsæ mechanica, saltèm digniores & puriores. Itaque hâc ratione philosophia & encyclopædia sunt synonyma. De istâ latissimâ acceptione loquitur Aristoteles 1. Metaph. c. 1. quando ait, omnes artium inuentores esse σοφούς, siue sapientes, etiamsi artes istæ sint manualariæ: quia nempe inuentores artium istarum igneâ quadam ingenij vi præ aliis hominibus fuerunt præditi, quâ causas rerum indagarunt: quod est philosophi. Atque huc etiam pertinent quinque illæ proprietates, glorioso profusâ & honorificæ, quas Aristoteles passim tribuit philosopho. Proprietates istæ sunt: 1. Omnia scire, accurate scilicet, & plene, quid esset sciri potest. 2. Posse docere res illas, quas scit. Signum quippe scientis est, posse docere. 3. Omnia scire & docere tum sui ipsius, tum doctrinæ causâ. Nam qui lucri aut quæstûs gratia docet, est mercenarius; nec est sophus, sed sophista. 4. Esse liberum; ita ut non suspendat sententiam suam vel à populari aurâ & favore, vel ab illorum, qui magnam sibi compararunt authoritatem iudicio: sed ut de rebus iudicet ex rerum naturâ: propriâque conscientiâ, non vero ex servili obsequio aliarum opinionum. Turpe siquidem est, intellectum suum, quem Deus constituit rerum humanarum dominum, facere mancipium alienæ libidinis: uti Scaliger ex. 307. sect. 3. vocat intellectum. 5. Non esse humanum, sed divinum & honorarium; quia philosphia est divinum & supra hominem positum negotium. Atque hæc est latissima philosophiæ significatio. Minus latè hæc vox sumitur duobus modis. 1. pro studio sapientiæ terrenæ & naturalis: ita ut complectatur etiam Iurisprudentiam & Medicinam. 2. pro omnibus disciplinis, quæ partim à tribus facultatibus superioribus, Theologiâ videl. Medicinâ & Iurisprudentiâ, partim ab artibus mechanicis distinguuntur. Et hoc modo philosophia continet non solum scientias & prudentias, sed etiam artes: siue, vt alij loquuntur, complectitur disciplinas obiectiuas & reales, quæ veniunt nomine τῶν θεωρητικῶν; ítémque directiuas seu organicas, quæ veniunt nomine αἱ πρακτικαί. Atque hoc modo Plato & Stoici accepiunt hanc vocem; illi quidem sæpe, isti semper. Secundo loco philosophiæ vocabulum accipitur idòς specialiter, & quidem quinque modis. 1. Pro compage disciplinarum realium, exclusis instrumentalibus. Et haec acceptio est Peripatetica. 2. Pro primâ philosophiâ seu metaphysicâ, quæ 1. Metaph. cap. 3. & lib. 11. c. 3. & 4. νοητῆ θεωρία dicitur philosophia. 3. Pro philosophiâ practicâ, seu doctrinâ vitæ bene honestèque instituendæ. Et hæc acceptio est Platonica. Hoc quoque sensu Iurisprudentia dicitur philosophia. 4. Pro Theologiâ; quam nonnulli vocant unam, veram, & divinam philosophiam; quod verissima sit sapientia, sine quâ sapientia humana sit larvata, & pura stultitia. 5. Pro Medicina & speciatim eâ, quæ est perfectior & excellentior. Hæc enim multis dicitur philosophia, & Severino in ideâ medicinâ, philosophia adeptâ, id est, perfectâ. Sic enim Scholastici accipiunt vocem illam adeptus, ut videre licet apud Scaliger. exer. 307. sect. 19. ubi notat intellectum adeptum dici, qui omnia actu intelligit. Hic quoque non est silentio prætereundum, philosophiæ vocabulum pro sectâ quoque accipi: ut quando Iosephus 2. de bello Iud. cap. 7. dicit, tria esse genera philosophiæ, seu tres philosophias, id est, sectas, apud Iudæos: videl. Pharisæos, Sadducæos, & Essæos.In every legitimate discussion, an explanation of the word opens the doors, and specifically the liberation of it from ambiguity. Let us, therefore, remove the ambiguity of this word, which is φιλοσοφία (philosophy) and τῶν πολλαχῶς λεγομένων ("of the many things spoken"). Firstly, however, the term philosophy is taken generally, and indeed either most broadly or less broadly. Most broadly, it stands for all learning and doctrine, which can enter the mind of man. And in this way, it encompasses under itself all faculties, as they call them, and from Cicero's 2nd "On Duties" it is thus defined: Philosophy is the knowledge of divine and human things, and the causes by which these are contained. Compare the same in the 3rd "On the Orator," letter 1 "On Laws." Under this most broad significance are contained the mechanical arts themselves, at least the more dignified and pure. Thus, in this manner, philosophy and the encyclopedia are synonyms. Aristotle speaks of this most broad acceptance in the 1st Metaphysics, chapter 1, when he says that all inventors of arts are σοφούς (wise men) or sapientes, even if those arts are manual: because namely the inventors of those arts were endowed with a certain fiery force of ingenuity above other men, by which they investigated the causes of things: which is of the philosopher. And to this also pertain those five properties, gloriously ample and honorable, which Aristotle everywhere attributes to the philosopher. These properties are: 1. To know everything, accurately, namely, and fully, what can be known. 2. To be able to teach those things which he knows. For a sign of knowing is the ability to teach. 3. To know and teach everything both for his own sake and for the sake of the doctrine. For he who teaches for the sake of profit or gain is a mercenary; nor is he a sage, but a sophist. 4. To be free; so that he does not suspend his judgment either by the popular breeze and favor, or by the judgment of those who have acquired great authority for themselves: but to judge of things from the nature of things and his own conscience, not from servile obedience to other opinions. Indeed, it is disgraceful for the intellect, which God has established as the lord of human affairs, to become a slave of foreign lust: as Scaliger calls the intellect in example 307, section 3. 5. Not to be human, but divine and honorable; because philosophy is a divine business placed above man. And this is the broadest significance of philosophy. Less broadly, this word is taken in two ways. 1. For the study of earthly and natural wisdom: so that it also encompasses Jurisprudence and Medicine. 2. For all disciplines, which are partly distinguished from the three superior faculties, namely Theology, Medicine, and Jurisprudence, partly from the mechanical arts. And in this way, philosophy contains not only sciences and wisdoms but also arts: or, as others say, it encompasses objective and real disciplines, which come under the name τῶν θεωρητικῶν (the theoretics); likewise the directive or organic ones, which come under the name αἱ πρακτικαί (the practical). And in this way, Plato and the Stoics accept this word; indeed Plato often, the Stoics always. In the second place, the term philosophy is specifically accepted in five ways. 1. For the structure of real disciplines, excluding instrumental ones. And this acceptance is Peripatetic. 2. For first philosophy or metaphysics, which in the 1st Metaphysics chapter 3 and book 11 chapters 3 and 4 is called νοητῆ θεωρία (intellectual theory). 3. For practical philosophy, or the doctrine of life to be well and honorably set up. And this acceptance is Platonic. In this sense also, Jurisprudence is called philosophy. 4. For Theology; which some call the one, true, and divine philosophy; because it is the truest wisdom, without which human wisdom is masked and pure foolishness. 5. For Medicine and specifically that which is more perfect and excellent. For this is called philosophy by many, and by Severinus in the idea of Medicine, philosophy attained, that is, perfected. For the Scholastics understand the word adeptus in this way, as can be seen in Scaliger's exercise 307, section 19, where he notes that an adept intellect is said to understand everything in actuality. Here also it must not be passed over in silence, that the term philosophy is also accepted for a sect: as when Josephus in the 2nd "On the Jewish War," chapter 7 says that there are three kinds of philosophy, or three philosophies, that is, sects, among the Jews: namely the Pharisees, Sadducees, and Essenes.
VIII. Philosophiæ vocabulum authorem habet Pythagoram; & retinetur in scholis, non quòd itâ sit aptum, sed quia iam est receptum.VIII. The Term Philosophy Has Pythagoras as Its Author, and Is Retained in Schools Not Because It Is So Apt, but Because It Is Already Accepted.
Philosophiam dici à φιλεῖν & σοφία notum est: ita ut sit amor sapientiæ, quemadmodum Cic. 2. Offic. interpretatur. Allusiva igitur potiùs quàm exacta est illa notatio, quòd philosophus sit φύλαξ σοφίας, ita ut debeat scribi phylosophus. Porro σοφία & σοφὸς rectè deducuntur à σαφής, id est, clarus; quia sapientia est clara & distincta notitia, quâ rerum causæ cognoscuntur. Unde Aristot. 1. poster. analyt. c. 2. ait: Scire est rem per causas cognoscere. Et hoc est illud Urim & Tummim, quod in pectore Aharonis scriptum erat, nonnulli autumant: quia duabus illis vocibus significatur claritas & perfectio. Cæterum Pyrhagoras inuenit vocabulum philosophi, ut author est Diogen. Laërtius in viâ ipsius, & Cicero in proœm. 5. Tusc. Illi enim cum superbum nimis & insolens videretur nomen σοφός, sapientis, philosophum se appellari maluit: quod nimirum absoluta sapientia in solum Deum, in hominem vero sapientiæ studium & amor cadant. Ubi & illud nota, Pythagoram noluisse vocari magnum, ut Chaldæi, Persæ, & alii orientales philosophi nuncupabantur: neque σοφιστής, ut occidentales philosophi vocabantur, neque σοφὸς aut σοφιστικὸς, uti Græci orientaliores appellabantur: sed φιλόσοφος & distinctionis, & modestiae causâ. Verum enim vero utvt laudetur Pythagoræ modestia; rectius tamen factum fuisset, si arti nomen σοφίας relictum fuisset. Neque enim hic spectandum est quid homines possimus, aut non possimus, sed ipsa rei natura attendenda. Profecto si vires humani ingenii nomina debeant dare rebus, iam nulla virtus aut doctrina futura est, cui nomen φιλοσόψιον, non sit præponendum: nec iam amplius dicendum fuerit διακοσμῆσαι, sed φιλοδιακοσμῆσαι; nec θεολογία, sed φιλοθεολογία. Deinde, omnes possimus esse philosophi, i.e. amatores sapientiæ, etiam rustici & plebei; verum non omnes possimus esse σοφοί, id est, sapientes & indagatores causarum. Tametsi vero hæc vox non est usque adeo apta: non tamen ideo non usurpanda; cum notum sit illud, Vocabula valent rerum instar nummorum.It is known that philosophy is said to be from φιλεῖν (to love) and σοφία (wisdom): so that it is the love of wisdom, as Cicero interprets in the 2nd "On Duties." Therefore, that notation is more allusive than exact, that the philosopher is φύλαξ σοφίας (guardian of wisdom), so that it ought to be written as phylosophus. Moreover, σοφία (wisdom) and σοφὸς (wise) are rightly derived from σαφής (clear), because wisdom is clear and distinct knowledge by which the causes of things are known. Hence Aristotle in the 1st "Posterior Analytics," chapter 2, says: To know is to know a thing through its causes. And this is that Urim and Thummim, which was written on the breast of Aaron, some think: because by those two words clarity and perfection are signified. Moreover, Pythagoras invented the term philosopher, as Diogenes Laërtius reports in his life, and Cicero in the prologue to the 5th "Tusculans." For to him, the name σοφός (wise man) seemed too arrogant and presumptuous, so he preferred to be called a philosopher: because absolute wisdom belongs to God alone, while the study and love of wisdom belong to man. And there note that Pythagoras did not want to be called great, as the Chaldeans, Persians, and other Eastern philosophers were named: nor sophist, as Western philosophers were called, nor σοφὸς or σοφιστικὸς, as the more Eastern Greeks were named: but philosopher for the sake of distinction and modesty. But truly, although Pythagoras's modesty is praised; it would have been better, however, if the art had been left the name of wisdom. For here it is not to be regarded what men can or cannot do, but the very nature of things is to be attended to. Indeed, if the strengths of human ingenuity must give names to things, then there would be no virtue or doctrine to which the name φιλοσοφία (love of wisdom), should not be prefixed: nor should we say anymore διακοσμῆσαι (to adorn), but φιλοδιακοσμῆσαι (to love to adorn); nor θεολογία (theology), but φιλοθεολογία (love of theology). Then, we all can be philosophers, i.e., lovers of wisdom, even the rustic and common people; but not all can be σοφοί (wise), i.e., sages and investigators of causes. Although indeed this word is not altogether so apt, it nevertheless is not for that reason not to be used; since it is known that words are worth the same as coins.
IX. Philosophia tanquam ens collectivum, non potest perfectè definiri.IX. Philosophy, as a Collective Entity, Cannot Be Perfectly Defined.
Entia collectiva seu aggregata complectuntur res plurium prædicamentorum, vel res plures unius prædicamenti, specie distinctas, ideóque perfectam definitionem non admittunt. Exemplo sint mundus, exercitus, & sylva. Etenim hæc talia non habent unam per se essentiam. Definitio autem perfecta est unius essentiæ explicatio. Sed, inquis, philosophia est habitus; & disciplinæ, quas illa complectitur, sunt quoque habitus. In uno igitur prædicamento est philosophia, nempe qualitatis. Sed, inquam, philosophia est habitus mixtus ex variis habitibus essentiâ specificâ differentibus; quorum una communis & exacta ratio dari non potest. Vide Iulium Pacium in prolegom. super phys. Arist.Collective or aggregate entities encompass things of multiple categories or several things of a single category, distinct in species, and therefore do not admit a perfect definition. Examples are the world, an army, and a forest. Indeed, such things do not have a single essence on their own. A perfect definition, however, is an explanation of a single essence. But, you may say, philosophy is a habit; and the disciplines that it encompasses are also habits. Therefore, philosophy is in one category, namely quality. But I say, philosophy is a mixed habit composed of various habits differing in specific essence, of which one common and exact concept cannot be given. See Julius Pacius in the prolegomena on Aristotle's Physics.
X. Variæ definitiones philosophiæ inter se collatæ suppeditant ipsius definitionem perfectæ proximam.X. Various Definitions of Philosophy Compared Among Themselves Provide an Approximation of Its Perfect Definition.
Plurimæ circumferuntur definitiones philosophiæ: quas nonnulli simpliciter reiiciunt. Nos existimamus, collationem inter illas esse instituendam, ut hâc ratione perveniri possit ad definitionem, quæ sit quam proxima perfectæ. Colligamus igitur aliquot præcipuas saltem. Principio se nobis offert Plato, qui hinc inde has adfert definitiones: Philosophia est similitudo Dei, quatenus homini possibile est, ut Deo sit similis. Item, philosophia est notitia divinarum & humanarum rerum, causarumque quæ iis continentur. Quam definitionem Cic. 2. offic. & omnes Stoici retinuerunt. Toletus quoque & alij, Platonici in primis, contendunt, hanc definitionem esse omnium perfectissimam. Idem Plato ait: φιλοσοφία ἐστὶ μελέτη θανάτου, Philosophia est meditatio mortis. Quam definitionem Ammonius super Isagogen Porphyrii, recte sic explicat: quod meditatio mortis Platoni nihil sit aliud, quam raptus animæ, sive animæ intra corpus hæretis elevatio, atque adeò abstractio cogitationum à singularibus ad universalia, à materialibus ad immaterialia, à voluptate denique sensitiva ad practicam illam & intellectualem. Unde Platonici dicunt, philosophi veri munus esse meditari mortem, i.e. in hoc desudare, ut animum à corporearum voluptatum γοναῖς & commercio sevoceat atque seiungat. Nam illecebris voluptatum & corporis contagione animus avocatur à veri contemplatione, & boni actione. Atque hæc est ecstasis illa Platonica, quà fit, ut philosophus sæpenumero habeat oculos aperto, & non videat, id est, fræna non laxet affectibus. Alii philosophiam finiunt partum ignorantiae, & medicinam ejusdem: itemque medicinam, quae sanat morbos animi. Partus ignorantiae dicitur: quia ignoratio parit admirationem, admiratio inquisitionem & scientiam, atque adeò philosophiam. Hinc philosopari est effectorem, quae admiratur, causas, quas ignoramus, inquire. Nam quemadmodum in motu est terminus à quo, medium per quod, & terminus ad quem: ita in philosophiae studio est ignoratio causarum, à qua per admirationem & discursum provcheimur ad notitiam causarum, qua est vera philosophia. Quod autem dicitur philosophia sanare morbos animi, hoc ita est accipiendum, ne quod Theologiae proprium est, transferibatur philosophiae: quod quidem factum esse videtur in oratoriâ illâ philosophiae definitione apud Apuleium & Ciceronem: Philosophia est virtutis indagatrix, expultrixque vitiorum, divinarum particeps rerum. Quanquam si hæc definitio cum grano salis accipiatur, tribui quoque potest philosophiae: ita nimirum ut theologia maneat sol, philosophia luna. Arist. 2. Metaph. c. 1. ait: Philosophia est ἐπιστήμη ἀληθείας, scientia veritatis. Ubi philosophus loquitur de philosophiâ theoreticâ duntaxat. Cic. 1. de oratore, sic describit: Philosophia est omnium artium laudatarum fasciculus. Ubi nomen fasciculus valde conveniens est: quia exprimit naturam philosophiae, quatenus illa posita est in comburatione, collectione, & aggregatione diversarum rerum. Simplicio, celeberrimo illi Aristotelis interpreti, philosophia est perfectio animae humanae. Damasceno c. 3. & 65. Dialect. Philosophia est τέχνη τεχνῶν, ἐπιστήμη ἐπιστημῶν,ἐ τέχνη πάσῃς τέχνης, ars artium, & scientia scientiarum, & principium omnis artis. Sed veniamus ad recentiores: in quibus primo occurrit philomela illa Germania Philippus Melanchthon, cui philosophia est artium dicendi, physiologiae, & præceptorum de moribus cognitio tanta, quantam humana mens in hâc imbecillitate assequi potest. Petrus Martyr in prolegom. super Ethic. Arist. ait: Philosophia est habitus mentibus humanis à Deo concessus, industriâ & exercitatione auctus, quo comprehenduntur omnia, quae sunt, quae nempe certâ & firmâ ratione comprehendi possunt, ut ad felicitatem homo pervenire possit. Fortunatus Crellius in prolegom. super acroamatica Aristot. ait: Philosophia est habitus animi, qui scientiâ & prudentiâ constat. Keckermanno philosophia est apta compages scientiarum & prudentiarum. Alii philosophiam vocant collectionem ignis Promethei. Qua descriptio poetica ex Plutarcho explicari potest hunc in modum. Artes omnes sunt minutae φρονώδεις, sive rivuli & particulæ prudentiae, quibus vita nostra irrigatur. Qua particulae & fragmenta prædentiæ postquam certâ serie fuerint dispositæ, artis nomen adipiscuntur. Atque hi sunt igniculi, quos mortalibus divisit Prometheus. Qui igniculi collecti in unum corpus magnum constituunt ignem & flammam e quibus emicuerunt illustres artium arces. Ex his definitionibus, reiectis nempe reiciendis, nos extruximus talem: Philosophia est comprehensio liberalium disciplinarum inferiorum; quas alij vocant studia humanitatis. Comprehensio, inquam, vel scripto expressa, vel animo impressa. Illo modo philosophia est systema multorum systematum: isto est habitus mixtus, sive constans multorum habituum collectione.Numerous definitions of philosophy circulate, which some simply reject. We think a comparison between them should be made, so that in this way it might be possible to arrive at a definition that is as close as possible to the perfect one. Let us, therefore, gather at least a few of the main ones. First, Plato presents himself, who offers these definitions here and there: Philosophy is the likeness of God, as far as it is possible for a man to be similar to God. Also, philosophy is the knowledge of divine and human things, and of the causes that are contained in them. Cicero in the 2nd "On Duties" and all the Stoics maintained this definition. Toletus and others, especially the Platonists, contend that this definition is the most perfect of all. Plato also says: φιλοσοφία ἐστὶ μελέτη θανάτου, Philosophy is the meditation on death. Ammonius on Porphyry's Isagoge explains this definition correctly as follows: that the meditation on death for Plato is nothing else but a rapture of the soul, or the elevation of the soul that clings to the body, and indeed an abstraction of thoughts from particulars to universals, from materials to immaterials, and finally from sensual pleasure to that practical and intellectual one. Hence the Platonists say that the task of the true philosopher is to meditate on death, i.e., to labor at this, to withdraw and separate the soul from the snares and commerce of bodily pleasures. For the mind is distracted from the contemplation of the truth and the action of good by the allurements of pleasures and the contagion of the body. And this is the Platonic ecstasy, by which it happens that the philosopher often has open eyes and does not see, that is, does not loosen the reins to the affections. Others define philosophy as the offspring of ignorance and its medicine; likewise, medicine that heals the diseases of the soul. It is called the offspring of ignorance because ignorance gives birth to admiration, admiration to inquiry and knowledge, and thus to philosophy. Thus to philosophize is to seek the causes of things we admire and which we do not know. For just as there is a starting point in motion, a medium through which, and an endpoint to which, so in the study of philosophy there is ignorance of causes, from which through admiration and discourse we are led to the knowledge of causes, which is true philosophy. But when it is said that philosophy heals the diseases of the soul, this is to be understood so that what is proper to Theology is not transferred to philosophy: which indeed seems to have been done in that rhetorical definition of philosophy in Apuleius and Cicero: Philosophy is the investigator of virtue and expeller of vices, participant in divine matters. Although if this definition is taken with a grain of salt, it can also be attributed to philosophy: so namely that theology remains the sun, philosophy the moon. Aristotle in the 2nd Metaphysics, chapter 1, says: Philosophy is ἐπιστήμη ἀληθείας, the science of truth. Where the philosopher speaks only of theoretical philosophy. Cicero in the 1st "On the Orator" describes it thus: Philosophy is the bundle of all praised arts. Where the name bundle is very appropriate because it expresses the nature of philosophy, as far as it is placed in the burning, collecting, and aggregating of various things. For Simplicius, that most celebrated interpreter of Aristotle, philosophy is the perfection of the human soul. For Damascenus in chapters 3 and 65 of Dialectic, philosophy is τέχνη τεχνῶν, ἐπιστήμη ἐπιστημῶν, the art of arts, and the science of sciences, and the principle of every art. But let us come to the more recent: among whom first occurs that songstress of Germany, Philipp Melanchthon, for whom philosophy is the knowledge of the arts of speaking, physiology, and the precepts of morals, as much as the human mind can achieve in this weakness. Peter Martyr in the prolegomena to Aristotle's Ethics says: Philosophy is a habit granted to human minds by God, increased by industry and exercise, by which are comprehended all things that are, which, namely, can be comprehended by sure and firm reasoning, so that man can attain happiness. Fortunatus Crellius in the prolegomena to Aristotle's acroamatics says: Philosophy is a habit of the mind, which consists of knowledge and wisdom. For Keckermann, philosophy is the apt structure of sciences and wisdoms. Others call philosophy the collection of Prometheus's fire. Which poetic description can be explained from Plutarch in this manner. All the arts are small insights, or streams and particles of wisdom, by which our life is irrigated. Which particles and fragments of wisdom, after they have been arranged in a definite order, obtain the name of art. And these are the sparks, which Prometheus distributed to mortals. These collected sparks form a great body of fire and flame from which illustrious heights of the arts have shone forth. From these definitions, rejecting those that should be rejected, we have constructed such a one: Philosophy is the comprehension of the lower liberal disciplines; which others call studies of humanity. Comprehension, I say, either expressed in writing or impressed upon the mind. In this way, philosophy is a system of many systems; in that way, it is a mixed habit, or a constant collection of many habits.
XI. In ordine disciplinarum philosophicarum constituendo magnum est operae pretium varias variorum authorum partitiones inter sese conferre, & illam reliquis anteferre, quae convenit cum subjecto, objecto, & fine Philosophiae.XI. In establishing the order of philosophical disciplines, it is very useful to compare the various divisions of various authors among themselves and to prefer that which agrees with the subject, object, and purpose of Philosophy.
Philosophia est totum & quidem collectium, cujus natura plene non potest intelligi sine partitione commoda. Hanc autem tum demum consequemur, si primo veterum & recentiorum philosophorum celebriores distributiones breviter colligamus, & deinde illam seligamus, quae cum subjecto, objecto, & fine Philosophiae convenit. Principio, quod ad vetustas philosophiae partitiones attinet, occurrit partitio Zenonis & Chrysippi, atque adeo Stoicorum, qui duos istos authores praecipue secuti sunt: de quibus refert Laërtius lib. 7. de vitiis philosophorum, quod philosophiam partiti sint in Logicam, Physicam, & Ethicam. Logicam dixerunt esse septum universae philosophiae contra insultus protervonum sophistarum: Physicam compararunt horto, in quo flores & arbores omnis generis visuntur: Ethicam assimilarunt coelo, quod secunderet illum hortum. Dixerunt etiam philosophiam esse quondam quasi animal, cujus ossibus similis sit Logica, carnibus Ethica, animae Physica; item velut urbem, in qua Logica vicem murorum, Physica civium, Ethica legum subeat. Hanc tamen divisionem Cicero 1. Acad. quaest. refert ad Platonem his verbis: Fuit, inquam, a Platone philosophiae accepta ratio triplex: una de vita & moribus; altera de natura & rebus occultis; tertia de disserendo. Et de Orat. ad Q. frat. ait: Omnis philosophia in tres partes est distributa, in naturae obscuritatem, in differendi subtilitatem, in vitam atque mores. Sed quia hae divisio non occurrit in scriptis Platonis, Cicero forte huc respexit, quod omnes Platonis dialogi aut sunt morales, ut est Alcibiades aut naturales, ut est Timæus, aut dialectici, ut est Parmenides. In scriptis autem Platonis haec occurrit philosophiae partitio, quod sit vel propagéa, vel paedia. Propagéa est, quae praeparat mentem ad graviores disciplinas & completur artes stricté dietas. Paedia complexitur ipsas scientias & prudentias. Ex Ciceronis partitionibus oratoriis & extremo libro topicorum ad Trebatium, talis fere colligitur technologia: quod disciplinae sint vel definitae & speciales, vel indefinitae & generales. Ad recentes authores quod attinet, praecipuae illorum technologiae hae sunt, Celeberrima est partitio, qua philosophia dividitur in Naturalem, Moralem, & Dialecticam. Vide Collegium Conimbr. in prooem. phys. Arist. & Possenium lib. 2. biblioth. c. 3. Huic succedit illa, qua philosophicae disciplinae dividuntur in theoreticas, & practicas: & practicae in morales, quae dirigunt actiones voluntatis, & efficientes sive poeticas, quae dirigunt actiones intellectus: & hae subdividuntur in internas sive liberales, & externas sive mechanicas, quarum quatuor traduntur divisiones. Nam primo aliae praeparant materiam, ut quae metalla purgat; aliae opus conficiunt, ut quae arma fabricat; aliae confecto utuntur opere, ut Militaris. Deinde, aliae sunt principales, & quasi dominae aliarum, ut Architectura; aliae famulantes & subiectae, aut Caementaria. Tertio, aliae post actionem nullum confectum opus relinquunt, ut ars citharistarum; aliae relinquunt, quarum tria genera traduntur. Nam aliae nova conficiunt, ut figulorum ars; aliae collapsa restaurant, ut Chirurgica: aliae utrumque praestant, ut ars aedificandi. Quarto, quaedam ad necessaria subministranda comparatae sunt, ut Pistoria: aliae melius aut iucundius degendi gratia, ut Gladiatoria, ad vitam tuendam, ad delicias Unguentaria. Tertio nonnulli disciplinas philosophicas dividunt in superiores, uti sunt Morales scientiae & Naturales; & inferiores, uti sunt septem artes liberales, quas dividunt in trivium & quadrivium. Ibi sunt Grammatica, Rhetorica & Dialectica; hic Arithmetica, Geometria, Astrologia, & Musica. Quarto sunt qui dividant philosophiam in partem organicam, & inorganicam. Illa complectitur artes stricte sumto vocabulo: haec dividitur in speculativam & activam. Cum qua partitione coincidit illa, qua philosophia dividitur in disciplinas directivas, (quas alii appellant normales & instrumentales) & objectivas seu reales. Dividitur etiam philosophia eodem sensu in partem generalem, quae sub se habet artes; & specialem, quae continet scientias & prudentias. Alii dividunt philosophiam in partem theoreticam, & practicam, exclusis artibus, quas dicunt non esse partes, sed instrumenta philosophiae. Quae sententia est Keckermanni lib. 1. praecog. philos. cap. 2. Porro Timplerus c. 3. technologia artem liberalem subdividit in principalem, & minus principalem: principalem in theologicam & philosophicam; minus principalem vocat philologicam. Toletus in prolegom. ad organum dividit philosophiam in tres partes. Speculativam, Activam, & Factuicam. Ramei nonnulli faciunt alias artes orationis, alias rationis. Ex his omnibus tum vetustis tum recentibus philosophiae divisionibus, ea est omnium optima, quae disciplinas philosophicas (late nimirum accepto philosophiae vocabulo) partitur in theoreticas, practicas, & poeticas: vel si mavis dichotomiam, quae philosophiam dividit in theoreticam & practicam: & hanc subdividit in activam & factuicam. Haec enim partitio convenit cum subjecto, objecto, & fine philosophiae. Convenit etiam cum regulis Logicae, & denique probatur classicis philosophis. Nam primo subjectum informationis in philosophia est hominis anima, cujus tria cernuntur esse munia, videlicet contemplari, agere aliquid ex praecepto virtutis, & facere ea quae hanc vitam reddunt faciliorem. Cum ergo hominis anima sit id ipsum, cui destinata est philosophia omnis (unde Platonici philosophia est ἀρεταὶς θεωρητικαὶ τῆς ψυχῆς) utique necesse est, philosophiam & eius partes conformari ac distribui ratione officiorum animae: ut nimirum quaedam philosophiae pars sit theorica, quae doceat modum clare cognoscendi; alia practica, quae doceat modum bene & beateque vivendi; alia denique poetica, quae doceat modum facile discendi & theoriam & praxim. Deinde objectum sive subjectum tractationis (quod dici solet externum, sicut prius illud subjectum dicitur internum) in philosophia sunt res ipsae, quae ad duas classes quam rectissime reducuntur, ut nempe aliae sint necessariae, aliae contingentes. Res necessariae sunt Deus, & quas Deus produxit, ita ut homo nulla prudentia vel arte possit illas producere, sed tantum contemplari. Aliter dicuntur res immutabiles, quia ab homine mutari non possunt. Ita nullus homo, ut ut summus monarcha, potest efficere coelum, terram, ventum; neque potest impedire ventum vel pluviam. Res contingentes sunt, quas homo non tantum potest cognoscere, sed etiam efficere, quando vult: vi officii temperantiae & liberalitatis homo non tantum potest cognoscere, sed etiam sua voluntate efficere, id est, potest temperanter & liberaliter agere. Ita quoque potest domum & familiam instituere, rempublicam gubernare. Vnde hae res dicuntur mutabiles. Sunt autem duum generum res istae contingentes: videlicet actiones virtutis, & opera artium liberalium (de operibus servilibus hic non tractamus) e quibus illae concernunt voluntatem, ista intellectum poeticum. Cum ergo omnis res, quae in philosophia tractatur, sit vel tantum cognoscibilis, vel cognoscibilis & producibilis simul, & quidem producibilis vel per prudentiam, vel per artem, ideo recte philosophia dividitur in contemplativam, quae tractat de rebus necessariis, & activam, quae explicat actiones virtutis; & factitiam, quae tractat de actionibus artificialibus. Sed, inquis, Geometria & omnino disciplinae mathematicae tractant de rebus, quae fieri possunt humana opera, videlicet de linea. Sed, inquam ego, hoc fit ratione poetices, quae comitatur universam mathesin. Tertio haec partitio confirmatur ex fine philosophiae, qui est cognitio veri, & actio boni. Cognitio veri est, quae ita versatur circa verum, ut in se ipsa acquiescat. Actio boni est vel moralis, vel artificialis. Cognitio veri quaeritur in philosophia contemplativa; actio boni moralis in practica, effectio boni artificialis in poetica. Contemplativa siquidem absolute spectata in eo sine quiescit, ut sciat: practica vero & poetica cognoscere docent, non ut ibi quiescamus, sed ut cognita exprimamus opere vel morali, vel artificiali. Praeterea haec partitio convenit cum regulis Logicis; quae neque latior est suo divisio, neque angustior, & habet partes inter se differentes. Tametsi enim in omni disciplina sit theoria & praxis, cognitio & usus simul: non tamen ideo omnis disciplina simul est theoretica & practica. Nam cognitio sive contemplatio est vel pura, vel mixta. Illa stricte dicitur, haec late. Ista est cognitio rei necessariae, quae non potest a nobis produci; haec est notitia cuiuslibet rei, sive illa sit necessaria, sive contingens. Et posteriori modo philosophia practica naturam, partes & proprietates virtutis, reip. &c. explicat, non ut illa sciantur, sed ut agantur. Sic quoque praxeos, sive usus vocabulum est ambiguum. Nam si late sumatur, omnis disciplina debet habere usum aliquem. Sed stricte usus respicit perfectionem moralem & artificialem: & ita philosophia theoretica non habet praxim. Ad extremum haec partitio patronos habet summos philosophos. Nam Aristoteles, teste Laërtio in eius vita, hanc philosophiæ partitionem ad mortem usque constantissime retinuit, & eius adminiculo auditores suos felicissime instituit. Peripatetici quoque philosophi, inprimis Theophrastus, Alexander Aphrodisæus, Themistius, Simplicius, Boëtius, & alij veteres philosophi, retinuerunt. Ubi memorabilis est pictura Boëtij, qui annis 500. post Christi nativitatem floruit, genere patritio natus, & legatione functus ad Gothos, qui in Italiam irrumpebant, a quibus est martyrio affectus. Nam nunc quidem ad communem sese hominum mensuram cohibebat, nunc vero pulsare cælum summi verticis cacumine videbatur, quae cum caput altius extulisset, ipsum etiam cælum penetrabat, respicientium hominum frustrabatur intuitum. Vestes erant tenuissimae fili, subtili artificio, indissolubili materia perfectæ, quas vel post adempto cognovis, suis manibus ipsa texuerat. Easdem quoque partes aliquis forte non inepte dixerit illuminare, purgare, & perficere. Dialectica enim dum artificio disserendi vera a falsis distinguit, animum affusa veritatis luce illustrat: moralis philosophia, dum mores componit, & vitiorum fibras evellit, purgat voluntatem: naturalis, dum altiori rerum abditarum intelligentia mentem exornat, perficit. Plura hanc in sententiam vide apud Philonem Judæum lib. de agricolis, Laërt. in Zenone, & Plotinum lib. 3. enneadis 1. de triplici ascensu ad mundum intelligibilem.Philosophy is a whole and indeed a collection, the nature of which cannot be fully understood without a suitable division. However, we will only achieve this if we first briefly gather the more famous distributions of ancient and recent philosophers, and then choose that which agrees with the subject, object, and purpose of Philosophy. To begin with, concerning the ancient divisions of philosophy, the division of Zeno and Chrysippus, and indeed of the Stoics, who especially followed these two authors, is noted: Laërtius recounts in book 7. about the defects of philosophers, that they divided philosophy into Logic, Physics, and Ethics. They said Logic was the fence of all philosophy against the attacks of insolent sophists; Physics they compared to a garden, in which flowers and trees of all kinds are seen; Ethics they likened to the sky, which nourished that garden. They also said that philosophy was like an animal, whose bones are akin to Logic, flesh to Ethics, and soul to Physics; likewise like a city, in which Logic performs the role of walls, Physics of citizens, Ethics of laws. Cicero, nevertheless, attributes this division to Plato in Acad. quaest. 1 with these words: There was, I say, a threefold plan of philosophy received from Plato: one concerning life and morals; another about nature and hidden things; the third about debate. And in de Orat. to Quintus, the brother, he says: All philosophy is distributed into three parts: into the obscurity of nature, into the subtlety of debating, into life and morals. But because this division does not occur in the writings of Plato, Cicero perhaps had regard to this, that all of Plato's dialogues are either moral, such as Alcibiades, or natural, such as Timaeus, or dialectical, such as Parmenides. However, in Plato's writings, this division of philosophy occurs, that it is either propagéa or paedia. Propagéa is that which prepares the mind for more serious disciplines and is completed by arts strictly spoken. Paedia encompasses the sciences and prudences themselves. From Cicero’s rhetorical partitions and the last book of topics to Trebatius, a technology is thus nearly gathered: that disciplines are either defined and special, or undefined and general. Regarding recent authors, their chief technologies are these: the most renowned division, by which philosophy is divided into Natural, Moral, and Dialectical. See the Coimbra College in the preface to Aristotle’s Physics and Possevin in book 2. of the library, chapter 3. This is succeeded by that whereby philosophical disciplines are divided into theoretical and practical: and practical into moral, which direct the actions of the will, and effectual or poetic, which direct the actions of the intellect; and these are subdivided into internal or liberal, and external or mechanical, whose divisions are fourfold. Firstly, some prepare the material, as purifying metals; others accomplish the work, as manufacturing weapons; others use the completed work, as Military. Then, some are principal and almost masters of others, such as Architecture; others are subservient and subjected, or Masonry. Thirdly, some leave no completed work after the action, such as the art of guitar players; others do, of which three kinds are handed down. For some create new things, like the art of potters; others restore the collapsed, like Surgery; others provide both, like the art of building. Fourthly, some are arranged for providing necessities, as Baking; others for a better or more pleasant life, as Gladiatorial games, to sustain life, and for delight, Perfumery. Thirdly, some divide philosophical disciplines into superior, such as Moral and Natural sciences; and inferior, such as the seven liberal arts, which are divided into the trivium and quadrivium. There are Grammar, Rhetoric & Dialectic; here Arithmetic, Geometry, Astrology, & Music. Fourthly, some divide philosophy into an organic part and inorganic. The former encompasses arts in the strict sense of the term: the latter is divided into speculative and active. This division coincides with that by which philosophy is divided into directive disciplines (others call them normative and instrumental) and objective or real. Philosophy is also divided in the same sense into a general part, which comprises arts; and a special part, which contains sciences and prudences. Others divide philosophy into theoretical and practical parts, excluding arts, which they say are not parts but instruments of philosophy. This is Keckermann’s view in book 1. of preliminary philosophy, chapter 2. Furthermore, Timpler in chapter 3 of technology subdivides liberal art into principal and less principal: the principal into theological and philosophical; the less principal he calls philological. Toletus in the prolegomena to the organ divides philosophy into three parts: Speculative, Active, and Technical. Some followers of Ramus make some arts of speech, others of reason. From all these divisions of philosophy, both old and recent, the best of all is that which divides philosophical disciplines (with the term philosophy indeed broadly accepted) into theoretical, practical, and poetic: or if you prefer a dichotomy, which divides philosophy into theoretical and practical: and subdivides the latter into active and constructive. For this division agrees with the subject, object, and purpose of philosophy. It also agrees with the rules of Logic, and is ultimately approved by classical philosophers. For firstly, the subject of instruction in philosophy is the soul of man, which is perceived to have three duties, namely to contemplate, to act upon the precept of virtue, and to do those things which make this life easier. Since therefore the human soul is that to which all philosophy is destined (hence Platonic philosophy is "theoretical virtues of the soul"), assuredly it is necessary that philosophy and its parts be arranged and distributed according to the duties of the soul: namely that a part of philosophy should be theoretical, which teaches the way of clear understanding; another practical, which teaches the way of living well and happily; and finally another poetic, which teaches the way of easily acquiring both theory and practice. Secondly, the object or subject of discussion (which is usually called external, whereas the former subject is called internal) in philosophy are things themselves, which can be very rightly reduced to two classes, such that some are necessary, others contingent. Necessary things are God and what God has produced, such that man can produce them by no wisdom or art, but only contemplate them. They are otherwise called immutable things, because they cannot be changed by man. Thus no man, however great a monarch, can create heaven, earth, wind; nor can he prevent wind or rain. Contingent things are those which man can not only know but also produce when he wishes: by the office of temperance and liberality man can not only know, but also by his will produce, that is, he can act temperately and liberally. Likewise, he can establish a home and family, govern a state. Hence these things are called mutable. However, these contingent things are of two kinds: namely, actions of virtue and works of the liberal arts (we do not treat here of servile works) of which the former concern the will, the latter the poetic intellect. Since therefore every thing which is treated in philosophy is either only knowable, or knowable and producible at the same time, and indeed producible either through prudence or through art, hence philosophy is rightly divided into contemplative, which deals with necessary things, and active, which explains actions of virtue; and constructive, which deals with artificial actions. But, you ask, Geometry and all mathematical disciplines deal with things that can be made by human work, namely lines. But, I say, this is done by reason of poetry, which accompanies the entire mathematics. Thirdly, this division is confirmed by the purpose of philosophy, which is the knowledge of truth and the action of good. The knowledge of truth is that which revolves around the truth in such a way that it rests in itself. The action of good is either moral or artificial. The knowledge of truth is sought in contemplative philosophy; the action of moral good in practice, the production of artificial good in poetry. Contemplative indeed, absolutely considered, rests in knowing: while practical and poetic teach to know, not that we may rest there, but that we may express the known by moral or artificial work. Moreover, this division agrees with the logical rules; which neither has a division broader than its own, nor narrower, and has parts different from one another. For although in every discipline there is theory and practice, knowledge and use together: yet not every discipline is therefore both theoretical and practical at the same time. For the knowledge or contemplation is either pure or mixed. The former is strictly called, the latter broadly. The former is the knowledge of a necessary thing, which cannot be produced by us; the latter is the knowledge of any thing, whether necessary or contingent. And in the latter way, practical philosophy explains the nature, parts, and properties of virtue, the republic, etc., not that they may be known, but that they may be enacted. Thus also the term of practice or use is ambiguous. For if taken broadly, every discipline should have some use. But strictly, use regards moral and artificial perfection: and so theoretical philosophy has no practice. Finally, this division has the support of the greatest philosophers. For Aristotle, as testified by Laërtius in his life, consistently retained this division of philosophy until his death, and with its aid most successfully educated his listeners. The Peripatetic philosophers, particularly Theophrastus, Alexander of Aphrodisias, Themistius, Simplicius, Boethius, and other ancient philosophers, also retained it. Notably, there is the account of Boethius, who flourished around the year 500 after the birth of Christ, born of patrician descent, and serving as an envoy to the Goths who were invading Italy, by whom he suffered martyrdom. Boethius was the first to write the philosophy of Aristotle in Latin, and at the beginning of his commentary on "The Consolation of Philosophy," he graphically describes its division thus: While I was silently reflecting on this within myself, and designating a most tearful complaint in the role of a pen, a woman appeared to stand before me, with a very venerable face, eyes burning and more penetrating than the common strength of men, with a lively color and of inexhaustible vigor, although she was so full of herself that she would not in any way be believed of our age, of ambiguous stature of elegance. For now she indeed confined herself to the common measure of men, and now, however, she seemed to strike the sky with the top of her head, and when she lifted her head even higher, she penetrated the very heavens, frustrating the gaze of those beholding her. Her garments were of the thinnest thread, made by subtle art, perfected by indissoluble material, which, as you later learned, she had woven with her own hands. A certain fog of neglected antiquity had covered the appearance of these, as smoky images are accustomed. On the far margin of these was read 𝛼, indeed on the uppermost 𝛍 was inscribed. And between the two letters, in the manner of stairs, certain steps seemed distinguished, by which ascent was from the lower to the upper element. Yet certain violent hands had torn this same garment, and each took away any pieces they could. In her right hand she indeed held books, while in the left a scepter. From this image of philosophy, it appears what kind of division Boethius approved of, namely that in which philosophy is divided into theoretical and practical, under which we understand poetic. For by 𝛼 he understands theoretical, by 𝛑 he understands practical. The Academics and Stoics also approved of this threefold division of philosophy. For, as we saw a little before from Cicero, a threefold concept of philosophy was received from Plato: one concerning life and morals, another concerning nature and hidden things; the third concerning discourse, and what is true, what is false, what is right or crooked in oration, what is consistent, what is contradictory by judging. Hence they divided philosophy into Ethics or Morals, Physics or Natural, and Logic. Where you should remember, the words are to be taken broadly; so that Ethics encompasses prudences, Physics sciences, Logic all and singular arts. Augustine also approves of this very division in book 8 of "The City of God," chapter 4, and book 1, chapter 25, where he discusses almost in this manner: Just as there are three things considered in any craftsman as he accomplishes something; nature, doctrine, use; of which the first is judged by talent, the second by knowledge, the third by experience: so a threefold discipline had to be found by philosophers: Natural because of nature. Rational because of doctrine. Moral because of use. And since man is created by God to reach that which is superior in all by that which excels in himself, through this kind of threefold knowledge he reaches the one, true, most excellent God, without whom no nature subsists, no doctrine instructs, no use is beneficial. Thus, according to Augustine's opinion, these three parts of philosophy are like three steps, by which true philosophers strive to approach closer to the likeness of God. Someone could also aptly call these same parts illuminating, purifying, and perfecting. For Dialectic, while distinguishing truth from falsehood through the craft of arguing, enlightens the mind with the light of truth that is spread; moral philosophy, while it arranges morals, and plucks out roots of vices, purifies the will; natural philosophy, while it adorns the mind with a higher understanding of hidden things, perfects it. See more on this opinion in Philo Judeaus's book "On Farmers," Laërtius on Zeno, and Plotinus book 3, Ennead 1, on the triple ascent to the intelligible world.
XII. Philosophia theoretica informat hominis intellectum theoreticum: practica voluntatem & intellectum practicum: poetica intellectum poeticum. Et practica est omnium nobilissima, quatenus habet rationem finis.XII. Theoretical philosophy informs the theoretical intellect of man; practical philosophy informs the will and practical intellect; poetic philosophy informs the poetic intellect. And the practical is the most noble of all, insofar as it has the nature of an end.
De contentionem philosophiae theoreticae & practicae dici non potest quam anceps sit disputatio, utra nempe sit praestantior. Quae controversia ut decidatur, opus est distinctione. Philosophiae theoreticae subiectum informationis est intellectus theoreticus, subiectum tractationis sive doctrinae est veritas. Philosophiae autem practicae subiectum informationis est voluntas, itemque intellectus practicus: subiectum tractationis est bonitas moralis. Iam vero intellectus prior est voluntate. Intellectus enim portat voluntati lucernam: unde illud domino, haec dominae; ille soli, haec lunae comparatur. Intellectus quoque theoreticus est prior practico: quia praxis praesupponit cognitionem. Verum quoque prius est bono: quia hoc fundatur in illo. Hactenus ergo theoretica est nobilior practica quatenus haec ab illa dependet. Sed rursus philosophia practica est praestantior quam theoretica, quatenus illa habet rationem finis, & haec rationem medii. Consideratur enim philosophia theoretica vel absolute, & ita contenta est nuda cognitione, atque propter se experitur: vel relatae, & ita ordinatur ad actionem boni. Nam ideo contemplamur verum, ut eo expeditius aggrediamur actionem boni, & in ea felicius progrediamur. Quid multa? Philosophia theoretica, intellectus, & verum sunt praestantiora, quatenus habent rationem causae efficientis, a qua pendent philosophia practica, voluntas, & bonum. Sed philosophia practica, voluntas, & bonum sunt praestantiora, quatenus habent rationem finis, ad quem tanquam media ducunt & conducunt philosophia theoretica, intellectus, & verum. Atque hic finis perficit contemplationem: quia ex cognitione nuda (quae potest esse diabolica) facit cognitionem affectivam. Tametsi ergo philosophia theoretica est praestantior inchoative, ut ita dicam: practica tamen est praestantior terminative, quippe terminus & finis theoreticae. Et hoc sensu practicam philosophiam locamus in summo dignitatis gradu. Ita enim considerata parit summum hominis bonum, videlicet contemplationem Dei tanquam semper-patris iure creationis: quemadmodum fides in Christum parit summum bonum Christiani hominis, quod positum est in contemplatione Dei tanquam semper-patris iure redemptionis. In summa, Philosophia practica est nobilior quam theoretica, quatenus habet rationem finis, & est causa summi boni proxima: theoretica est nobilior, quatenus est efficiens practicae. Ita potentia naturalis, quatenus est causa operationis, est nobilior: quatenus operatio est finis potentiae, potentia effectus ignobilior. Potentia quippe est propter operationem. Præterea philosophia theoretica est praestantior ad scientiæ rationem, quia dignitas scientiæ, quâ scientia est, non ex fructu aut necessitate, sed ex modo, quo rem sibi objectam attingit, æstimatur: qui modus est eminentior in theoreticis, quam practicis; & ita scientia dicitur bonum honorabilius; sed philosophia practica est bonum utilius, quia nostra magis interest bene vivere, quam contemplari. Respectu quoque huius vel illius officii & personæ, nunc theoretica, nunc practica philosophia est eligibilior. Ad extremum contemplatio illa, quæ sequitur actionem boni, est nobilior ipsa actione. Sed hæc contemplatio est alius generis, quam illa quæ consideratur in philosophia theoretica. Et hoc sensu vita contemplativa est praestantior quam practica, quatenus nempe intelligitur contemplatio, quæ sequitur actionem virtutis; quæque vulgato dicitur contemplatio Seraphica.The contention between theoretical and practical philosophy cannot be discussed without recognizing how uncertain the dispute is, namely which is more excellent. To settle this controversy, a distinction is needed. The subject of information for theoretical philosophy is the theoretical intellect, while the subject of treatment or doctrine is truth. However, the subject of information for practical philosophy is the will, as well as the practical intellect; the subject of treatment is moral goodness. Indeed, the intellect is prior to the will. For the intellect casts a light before the will; whence that to the lord, this to the lady; the former is likened to the sun, the latter to the moon. The theoretical intellect is also prior to the practical, because practice presupposes knowledge. Truth is also prior to good, because good is founded upon truth. Therefore, up to this point, the theoretical is nobler than the practical, insofar as the latter depends on the former. But conversely, practical philosophy is more excellent than theoretical philosophy, insofar as the former has the nature of an end, and the latter the nature of a means. For theoretical philosophy is considered either absolutely, and thus it is content with naked knowledge, and experiences it for its own sake: or relatively, and thus it is ordained to the action of good. For we contemplate the truth in order to more readily undertake the action of good and progress more successfully in it. In short, theoretical philosophy, the intellect, and truth are more excellent, as they have the nature of an efficient cause, upon which practical philosophy, the will, and good depend. But practical philosophy, the will, and good are more excellent, as they have the nature of an end, to which theoretical philosophy, the intellect, and truth lead and conduct as means. And this end perfects contemplation, because from naked knowledge (which can be diabolical) it creates affective knowledge. Therefore, though theoretical philosophy is more excellent initiatively, so to speak, practical philosophy is more excellent terminatively, as it is the terminus and end of the theoretical. In this sense, we place practical philosophy in the highest degree of dignity. Considered in this way, it begets the highest good for man, namely the contemplation of God as the eternal Father by right of creation; just as faith in Christ begets the highest good for the Christian man, which is placed in the contemplation of God as the eternal Father by right of redemption. In sum, practical philosophy is nobler than theoretical, insofar as it has the nature of an end and is the proximate cause of the highest good: theoretical is nobler, insofar as it is the efficient cause of the practical. Thus, a natural power, as it is the cause of operation, is nobler; as operation is the end of power, the power resulting is less noble. For power is for the sake of operation. Furthermore, theoretical philosophy is superior regarding the nature of knowledge, because the dignity of knowledge, as it is knowledge, is not estimated from fruit or necessity, but from the manner in which it reaches its object; which manner is more eminent in theoretical than in practical matters; and thus knowledge is considered a more honorable good; but practical philosophy is a more useful good because it concerns us more to live well than to contemplate. With respect to this or that office and person, theoretical or practical philosophy may now be more desirable. Ultimately, that contemplation which follows the action of good is nobler than the action itself. But this contemplation is of a different kind than that considered in theoretical philosophy. And in this sense, the contemplative life is more excellent than the practical, insofar as it is understood as contemplation following the action of virtue; which is commonly called Seraphic contemplation.
XIII. Philosophiae theoretica partes distinguuntur pro varietate abstractionis.XIII. The parts of theoretical philosophy are distinguished according to the variety of abstraction.
Ratio est, quia diversam abstractionem necessario comitantur diversae scibilitates, ut loquuntur, id est, modi cognoscendi. Tot igitur sunt scientiae contemplativae, si capita ipsarum respicias, quot sunt abstractiones. Sunt autem tres in universum abstractiones. Prima abstractio est a materia sensili singulari: et haec est philosophi naturalis. Hic enim considerat hominem, ut ex animo et corpore organico constat, nec scrutatur materiam singularem, puta Petrum, Paulum. Secunda abstractio est a materia sensili communi, ratione tantum, non etiam re: et haec pertinet ad mathematicum. Quamvis enim mathematicus affectiones triangulorum, aliaque id genus contemplatur, quae in materia sensili haerent: non tamen, quatenus in eiusmodi materia sunt, expenderit: quia non considerat triangulum ligneum vel ferreum, sed triangulum in genere. Tertia denique abstractio est ab omni materia, et re, et ratione simul: quae pertinet ad Metaphysicam et Pneumaticam. Hae namque disciplinae in earum rerum cognitione versantur, quae neque in materia consistunt, neque illam in suo conceptu includunt: et proinde abstrahunt ab omni materia sensibili, sive communis illa sit, sive singularis, et a materia intelligibili. Quod autem diximus de abstractione a materia, similiter de separatione a motu intelligi volumus. Ex his efficitur, tres esse inspectricis philosophiae partes principes, metaphysicam, physicam, et mathematicam.The reason is that different abstractions are necessarily accompanied by different kinds of knowability, as it is said, that is, modes of knowing. Therefore, there are as many contemplative sciences, if you consider their heads, as there are abstractions. There are, however, three abstractions in total. The first abstraction is from singular sensible matter, and this belongs to the natural philosopher. For he considers man as consisting of both soul and organic body, and does not examine singular matter, such as Peter or Paul. The second abstraction is from common sensible matter, only in reason, not in reality: and this pertains to the mathematician. For although the mathematician contemplates the properties of triangles, and other such things, which adhere in sensible matter, he does not consider them insofar as they are in such matter: because he does not consider a wooden or iron triangle, but a triangle in general. The third abstraction is finally from all matter, both in reality and in reason simultaneously: which pertains to Metaphysics and Pneumatics. For these disciplines are engaged in the knowledge of those things which neither consist in matter nor include it in their concept: and therefore abstract from all sensible matter, whether it be common or singular, and from intelligible matter. What we have said about abstraction from matter is understood similarly about separation from motion. From these, it is understood that there are three principal parts of speculative philosophy: metaphysics, physics, and mathematics.
XIV. Quaedam philosophiae partes interdum hoc nomine non appellantur, ob quinque causas.XIV. Certain parts of philosophy are sometimes not called by this name, for five reasons.
Vel quia de rebus facilioribus, aut minoris momenti tractant: vel quia non sunt ipsam veritatis cognitione contentae: vel quia non videntur pertinere ad bene beateque vivendum: vel quia non agunt de rebus per se subsistentibus: vel quia non tradunt prima omnino principia rerum, de quibus agunt. Propter primam causam saepe mathematicae disciplinae non numerantur in partibus philosophiae. Aristot. 4. metaph. cap. 2. Plato 7. de rep. Cic. 1. de orat. Alexander initio commentar. in 1. analys. Propter secundam causam, nonnunquam solae etiam solae contemplativae disciplinae nomine philosophiae habentur dignae. Arist. 1. metaph. cap. 1. Propter tertiam interdum solae practicae significantur philosophiae vocabulo. Plato de philosophia. Propter quartam, saepè solae physicae scientiae habentur pro partibus philosophiae. Aristot. 4. metaphys. cap. 2. text. 4. Propter quintam non raro tribuitur philosophiae appellatio soli primae philosophiae. Aristot. 4. metaphysic. cap. 2. & cap. 3. text. 7. 8. Cum igitur his & aliis de causis videmus quasdam disciplinas a numero partium philosophiae excludi, non debemus existimare illas simpliciter reici ex regno philosophiae.Either because they deal with easier or less important matters; or because they are not content with the knowledge of truth itself; or because they do not seem to pertain to living well and happily; or because they do not concern things that subsist by themselves; or because they do not deliver the very first principles of the things they discuss. Due to the first reason, mathematical disciplines are often not included among the parts of philosophy. Aristotle, Metaphysics 4, chapter 2. Plato, Republic 7. Cicero, On the Orator 1. Alexander at the beginning of the commentary on the first Analytics. Due to the second reason, sometimes only the contemplative disciplines are considered worthy of the name of philosophy. Aristotle, Metaphysics 1, chapter 1. Due to the third reason, sometimes only the practical are signified by the term philosophy. Plato on Philosophy. Due to the fourth, often only physical sciences are regarded as parts of philosophy. Aristotle, Metaphysics 4, chapter 2, text 4. Due to the fifth, the name of philosophy is often attributed solely to first philosophy. Aristotle, Metaphysics 4, chapter 2 & chapter 3, texts 7 and 8. Therefore, when we see that certain disciplines are excluded from the number of parts of philosophy for these and other reasons, we should not consider them to be simply rejected from the realm of philosophy.

XVI. Ethica est prima inter disciplinas practicas: Politica dignissima.XVI. Ethics is the first among practical disciplines: Politics the most honorable.
Ethica in latifundio philosophiæ practicæ est prima, si quis animum attendat ad ordinem naturæ: quippe quæ est absoluta, & continet principia & quasi fundamenta reliquarum prudentiarum, agens de affectionibus animi, de virtutibus & illarum actionibus, de summo bono & ultimo fine hominis: quibus rebus non ante cognitis atque constitutis, facultas œconomica & politica, ne digitum quidem progredi possunt. Si verò spectes excellentiam, atque præstantiam, Politica prima tenet. Etenim ad hanc cæteræ, tanquam extremum & finem referuntur. Et hæc reliquas disciplinas ad civilem usum accommodat, adeóque practicè ordinat, ut Metaphysica illas ordinat theoretice. Œconomica quoque naturâ prior est quam civilis disciplina: veluti pars est prior toto, & unum multis. Scholastica proximè sequitur Politicam: quia schola est aliquid posterius politiâ. Contra hunc ordinem sic objicitur ex Aristotele. Idem est bonum hominis & ciuitatis. Ergo Ethica ad Politicam pertinet. Sed respondetur, Ethicam considerari absolutè, quatenus perficit hominem, vel relatè, quatenus homo est pars societatis politicae. Priori modo Ethica est prior Politicâ, posteriori modo ad Politicam, ut & Οἰκονομικὰν, refertur. Quo pacto etiam Οἰκονομικὰ pertinet ad Politicam.Ethics in the expanse of practical philosophy is the first, if one considers the order of nature: since it is absolute, and contains the principles and as if the foundations of the remaining prudences, treating of the affections of the mind, of virtues and their actions, of the highest good and the ultimate end of man: without the knowledge and establishment of which things, the economic and political capacities cannot advance even a finger. But if you look at excellence and distinction, Politics holds the first place. Indeed, the others are referred to this as to the ultimate and end. And this adapts the other disciplines to civil use, and thus practically arranges them, just as Metaphysics arranges them theoretically. Economics, too, by nature is earlier than civil discipline: just as a part is earlier than the whole, and one than many. Scholastic follows Politics closely: because the school is something subsequent to polity. Against this order, there is an objection from Aristotle. The good of man and of the state is the same. Therefore, Ethics pertains to Politics. But it is answered that Ethics is considered absolutely, insofar as it perfects man, or relatively, insofar as man is part of political society. In the former manner, Ethics is prior to Politics, in the latter manner it is referred to Politics, as is Economics. In this way, too, Economics pertains to Politics.
XVII. Metaphysica est prima, & nobilissima inter disciplinas theoreticas.XVII. Metaphysics is the first, and most noble of theoretical disciplines.
Prima quidem; quia generalissima. Nobilissima verò; quia immediatè imperat reliquis disciplinis theoreticis, constituendo illarum principia prima & subiecta denominata. Proximum dignitatis gradum obtinet Pneumatica: utpote quae tractat de spiritu increato & creato: qua tamen disciplina, quatenus de Deo tractat, nobilior est Metaphysicâ. Hanc excipit Physica: quia post tractationem spiritus proxima est tractatio corporis naturalis. Ultimo loco est Mathematica: quia tractatio accidentis posterior est tractatione substantiae. Cùm enim substantia in rebus primas teneat, præstantiores habentur ex scientiæ, quæ circa substantiam, quàm quæ circa accidentia versantur: & inter scientias prioris generis, quæ substantias à materia liberas considerant.First indeed; because most general. Most noble, however; because it immediately commands the remaining theoretical disciplines by establishing their first principles and subject denominations. The next rank of dignity is held by Pneumatics: as it treats of the uncreated and created spirit: which discipline, however, insofar as it treats of God, is more noble than Metaphysics. Physics follows this: because after the treatment of spirit, the treatment of natural body is next. Mathematics is in the last place: because the treatment of the accident is posterior to the treatment of substance. For since substance holds the first place in things, sciences that deal with substance are considered more excellent than those that deal with accidents: and among the sciences of the former kind, those that consider substances free from matter.
XVIII. Inter disciplinas poëticas prima est Lexica, nobilissima Logica.XVIII. Among poetic disciplines, Lexicography is the first, Logic the most noble.
Lexica prima est, quia tractat de vocum significatione. Necesse autem est, ut prius voces sciantur, quam res proponantur. A Lexicâ venitur ad Grammaticam, quia illius est explicare vocum adsignificationes puras. Hanc excipit Logica: quæ prior est quam Rhetorica, quod doctrina troporum omninò pendeat à Logicis. Rhetorica tamen priori loco commemoratur quam Logica, quæ est disciplina sermocinatirx, ideòque sub uno signo cum Lexicâ & Grammaticâ statuitur. Porro Oratoria differt à Rhetoricâ; quia Rhetorica duntaxat spectat ornatum orationis, Oratoria totum eloquentiæ campum sibi habet propositum. Poëtica sequitur Oratoriam; quia oratio ligata posterior est solutâ. Omnium tamen nobilissima est Logica; quia est directrix rationis.Lexicography is first because it deals with the meaning of words. It is necessary, moreover, that words be known before things are proposed. From Lexicography one comes to Grammar, because it is the one that explains the pure significations of words. Logic follows this: which is prior to Rhetoric, because the doctrine of tropes entirely depends on Logic. However, Rhetoric is mentioned before Logic, which is a discipline of discourse, and therefore is placed under one category with Lexicography and Grammar. Furthermore, Oratory differs from Rhetoric, because Rhetoric only regards the embellishment of speech, while Oratory has the whole field of eloquence proposed to itself. Poetics follows Oratory; because bound speech is later than free speech. Of them all, however, Logic is the most noble; because it is the guide of reason.
XIX. Metaphysica est postrema ordine inventionis.XIX. Metaphysics is last in the order of discovery.
Ratio est; quia Metaphysica est disciplinarum generalissima. Generalissima autem sunt ultima inventionis ordine. Deinde materiæ Metaphysicæ sunt admodum simplices. Res autem simplices a nobis non possunt cognosci, nisi adminiculo compositorum: quia nempe per abstractionem a nobis cognoscuntur. Per abstractionem dico a rebus materialibus & sensilibus. Hinc Physica & Mathematica rectè prædicantur priores Metaphysicâ, ordine inventionis.The reason is; because Metaphysics is the most general of the disciplines. The most general, however, are last in the order of discovery. Furthermore, the matters of Metaphysics are exceedingly simple. Simple things, however, cannot be known by us except with the help of composites: because indeed they are known by us through abstraction. By abstraction, I mean from material and sensible things. Hence Physics and Mathematics are rightly declared to be before Metaphysics in the order of discovery.
XX. Philosophia practica est prior ordine inventionis, quam theoretica.XX. Practical philosophy is prior in the order of discovery than theoretical.
Nam quia homo naturâ est animal politicum, & in societate primò vixit, impulsu naturæ conatus est primò mederi defectibus illis, qui accidunt in societate, atque ita peperit philosophiam practicam unà cum artibus servilibus, ut constat ex Genesis 4. Postea inventæ sunt artes theoreticæ, & omnium ultimo poëticæ liberales. Nam cùm homo commodè viveret in societate, cœpit animum adicere ad contemplationem operum Dei, unde Physica & Astronomia. Et quia in artibus mechanicis multi fuêre defectus, Mathematicas disciplinas excogitavit. Cùm autem videret se non ita bene posse progredi in contemplatione rerum, neque sensa animi cum aliis communicare, peperit Logicam & artes sermocinatrices.For since man is by nature a political animal and lived first in society, by impulse of nature he first attempted to remedy those defects which occur in society, and thus gave birth to practical philosophy along with servile arts, as is evident from Genesis 4. Theoretical arts were invented later, and last of all, the liberal poetic arts. For when man lived comfortably in society, he began to turn his mind to the contemplation of the works of God, from which came Physics and Astronomy. And because in mechanical arts there were many defects, he devised the Mathematical disciplines. But when he saw he could not progress so well in the contemplation of things, nor communicate his thoughts with others, he brought forth Logic and discursive arts.
XXI. Metaphysica accurata disciplinæ ordine prior est omnibus disciplinis: Physica prior est Mathematica: Philosophia theoretica universa est prior quam practica: omnium ultima est Philosophia poëtica.XXI. Metaphysics, in the order of precise discipline, is prior to all disciplines: Physics is prior to Mathematics: The entire theoretical philosophy is prior to the practical: the last of all is Poetic Philosophy.
Hoc ita patet. Quidquid in naturâ positum est, & lumine naturæ cognosci potest, id est, θεωρητικόν, πρακτικόν, vel ποιητικόν. Illud cadit sub contemplationem, qui est primus actus animæ: istud sub actionem boni moralis, qui est secundus actus; quia cognitionem veri sequitur actio boni honesti sive moralis; hoc sub affectionem sive actionem boni artificialis, qui est ultimus actus. Deinde τὸ ὄντως ὄν est vel generalissimum, ut ens, primus ille & summus conceptus, de quo Metaphysica: vel speciale, quod est vel purum, vel mixtum. Purum est substantia vel accidens. Substantia est vel increata, de quâ theologia; vel creata, & hæc vel spiritus, vel corpus. De illo tractat Pneumatica, de hoc Physica. Accidens primum est quantitas; unde Arithmetica & Geometria. Τὸ ὄντως ὄν speciale mixtum est corpus unà cum quantitate, vel qualitate. Ibi est Cosmographia, Uranometria, & Geographia; hic Optica & Musica. Tertio τὸ πράγματος consideteratur vel generaliter extra societatem, ut in Ethica; vel specialiter in societate, ut in Oeconomica, Politica, & Scholastica. Ultimo loco est τὸ ποιητικόν, quod respicit vel orationem, vel rationem. Quod respicit orationem & rationem, est vel purum & simplex, vel medium & mixtum. Illud respicit vel orationem tantum, vel rationem tantum: hoc utraque. In oratione est significatio, quam docet Lexica: vel adsignificatio, eaque tum pura, cuius magistra est Grammatica, tum ornata, cuius norma est Rhetorica. Rationem informat Logica. Orationem & rationem simul dirigunt Oratoria & Poëtica. Itaque universa philosophia dividitur in tres classes; e quibus prima classis subdividitur in decem familias; secunda classis habet quinque territoria; in tertia classe sunt sex provinciae; ita ut circulus sive regnum philosophicum constet XXI. disciplinis. Cæterum Metaphysica ordine doctrinæ exquisitæ est prima omnium disciplinarum: quia continet generalissimos terminos. Generalia autem sunt γνωριμώτερα φύσει, notiora naturâ, quemadmodum singularia sunt γνωριμώτερα ἡμῖν, notiora nobis. Ordo autem accuratæ doctrinæ consentaneus est ordini naturæ. Deinde Physica est prior Mathematicis exquisito doctrinæ ordine. Nam entia Mathematica pendent in suo esse a rebus naturalibus, in quibus existunt. Unaquæque autem res per ea perfecte intelligitur, quae eius esse pendant in suo esse. Et quantitas, primum accidens substantiæ corporeæ, non intelligitur perfecte nisi per substantiam. Porro sublato pronuntiato illo, Continuum esse divisibile in infinitum, corruit Geometria, quæ ubique supponit, a quolibet puncto in quemlibet punctum ducere lineam. Corruit etiam Arithmetica, quatenus ei subiectus est numerus materialium. Neque enim quantitas esset continua, nisi divideretur in semper divisibilia. Sublatâ autem quantitate continuâ nullus esset numerus rerum materialium. Præterea quia multa ex naturæ fontibus & physicae veritatis contemplatione ad moralem philosophiam necessario petenda sunt, ut quid sit anima, quid affectus, philosophiam practicam ordine accuratæ doctrinæ posteriorem esse theoreticâ necesse est. Ad extremum, quia disciplinæ poëticæ sive instrumentales occupantur circa notiones secundas & opera artificialia, quæ pendent a notionibus primis & contemplatione veritatis, merito illis tribuitur ultimus locus in ordine doctrinæ exquisitæ.This is evident as follows. Whatever is placed in nature and can be known by the light of nature is theoretical, practical, or poetic. The former falls under contemplation, which is the first act of the soul; the latter under the action of moral good, which is the second act, because the action of moral or honest good follows the cognition of truth; this under the affection or action of artificial good, which is the last act. Further, being truly is either general, like a being, that first and highest concept, which Metaphysics deals with, or special, which is either pure or mixed. The pure is substance or accident. Substance is either uncreated, of which theology speaks, or created, and this is either spirit or body. The former is treated by Pneumatics, the latter by Physics. The primary accident is quantity; hence Arithmetic and Geometry. The special mixed being is the body along with quantity, or quality. There is Cosmography, Uranometry, and Geography; here Optics and Music. Thirdly, the thing is considered either generally outside society, as in Ethics; or specifically in society, as in Economics, Politics, and Scholastics. In the last place is the poetic, which regards either speech or reason. What regards speech and reason is either pure and simple, or intermediate and mixed. The former regards either speech only, or reason only; the latter both. In speech, there is signification, which Lexicography teaches; or connotation, either pure, of which Grammar is the teacher, or adorned, of which Rhetoric is the guide. Logic informs reason. Oratory and Poetics together direct both speech and reason. Thus, the entire philosophy is divided into three classes; the first class is subdivided into ten families; the second has five territories; in the third, there are six provinces; so the circle or philosophical realm consists of twenty-one disciplines. Moreover, Metaphysics, in the order of refined doctrine, is first of all disciplines: because it contains the most general terms. General things are more knowable by nature, just as singular things are more knowable to us. The order of precise doctrine agrees with the order of nature. Further, Physics is prior to Mathematics in the refined order of doctrine. For mathematical entities depend on natural things for their existence, in which they exist. Each thing is perfectly understood through those things on which its existence depends. And quantity, the first accident of corporeal substance, is not perfectly understood except through substance. Furthermore, removing the proposition that the continuum is infinitely divisible, Geometry collapses, which everywhere supposes that from any point to any point, a line can be drawn. Arithmetic also collapses, insofar as it is subject to the number of material things. For quantity would not be continuous unless divided into always divisible parts. Without continuous quantity, there would be no number of material things. Furthermore, because much must necessarily be sought from the sources of nature and the contemplation of physical truth for moral philosophy, it is necessary for practical philosophy to be later than theoretical philosophy in the order of precise doctrine. Finally, because poetic or instrumental disciplines are occupied with secondary notions and artificial works, which depend on primary notions and the contemplation of truth, they rightfully hold the last place in the order of refined doctrine.
XXII. In ordine melioris doctrinæ eum locum obtinent disciplinæ, quem requirit imbecillitas nostra.XXII. In the order of better teaching, disciplines hold the place that our frailty requires.
Hunc ordinem ita circumscribimus. Gymnasia sunt vel παιδαγωγεία, vel gymnasmata. Illa minoribus erudiendis destinantur, hæc grandioribus & provectioribus tum ætate tum iudicio. In pædagogeis præter rudimenta pietatis docendæ sunt primò & discendæ artes orationis, Lexica, Grammatica, & pars Rhetoricæ: tum Logica, Oratoria & Poëtica: denique initia Arithmeticæ & Musicæ. In gymnasiis, quæ dicuntur gymnasmata, linguarum doctrina uberior tradenda, ideóque artes orationis & reliquæ in pædagogeis inculcatæ, hic repetendæ. Tum corpus totius philosophiæ, quasi per anatomen, confirmatis auditorum mentibus proponendum, ita quidem ut Mathematica pura, quæ vocantur, id est, Arithmetica & Geometria, primo loco tradantur: dehinc Physica adiiciantur, tum Pneumatica & Metaphysica. His denique succedat philosophia practica, tanquam colophon cursus philosophici, à quo transitus fiat ad facultatem aliquam summam. Rationem singulorum videamus. Principiò artes orationis ordine melioris doctrinæ sunt omnium primæ: quia disciplinæ nunc temporis non traduntur in linguâ vernaculâ, quemadmodum olim. Ubi notabis Rhetoricæ partem de figuris commodè proponi posse ante Logicam, tropologiam non item: eò quod hæc pendeat a cognitione argumentorum Logicorum. Deinde Mathematica pura ordine melioris doctrinæ prior est quam Physiologia; quia Mathematica iuvant ad perceptionem aliarum disciplinarum, & in iis addiscendis minus est negoti. Vnde propter addiscendi facilitatem, commune disciplinarum nomen (μαθήματα) fecere suum. At Physica cum reconditam naturæ vim scrutetur, & à vagâ atque errabundâ sensuum notitiâ maximam partem pendeat, longi temporis observationem requirit atque experientiam, proindeque multo est difficilior & operosior. Ideò veteres pueris suis explicarant mathemata: & Aristoteles 6. Eth. c. 8. ait, puerum fieri mathematicum, sapientem vel physicum non posse. Et Plato mathematicas disciplinas accenset μουσικῷδεῖ, illósque, qui mathematicis, ac nominatim Geometricis, imbuti non essent, à gymnasio suo arcuit, inscriptis Academiæ foribus hisce verbis: μηδεὶς ἀγεωμέτρητος εἰσίτω, Nemo Geometriæ ignarus huc ingrediatur. Tertiò Metaphysica & Pneumatica ordine melioris doctrinæ sunt omnium scientiarum postremæ. Nam quæ scientiæ res maximè abstrusas, & à sensuum consuetudine remotas tradunt, eæ postremo loco, propter nostram imbecillitatem, addiscendæ sunt. Ita verò se habent Metaphysica & Pneumatica. Adde, quod eorum, quæ in aliis scientiis pertractantur, notitia, ad primam philosophiam præparat animum & acuit, ut Augustinus 2. lib. de ordine edisserit, aiens, humaniam rationem, cum ad ea, quæ altiora sunt, agnosceret adspiraret, ne ascensum frustra moliretur, neve de alto caderet, interiores disciplinas sibi tanquam gradus pararet. Philosophus itaque per alias scientias gradatim ad Metaphysicam & Pneumaticam ascendit. Denique Philosophia practica ultimo loco discenda est, ordine melioris doctrinæ. Maturiori quippe opus est iudicio ad eorum intelligentiam, quibus componendi sunt mores, regenda familia, instituenda resp., quam ad capessendum eruditionem physicam & omnino theoreticam. Et in iis, quæ ad vitæ disciplinam, & regendas ac moderandas hominum voluntates pertinent, fallacior est & incertior, & magis ardua experientia, quam in theoreticis. Quo spectat illud Aristotelis 1. Ethic. c. 3. iuvenem haudquaquam esse idoneum moralis disciplinæ auditorem; quia nimirum in perturbationum regno non potest esse firmum de rebus agendis iudicium, dumque reciprocnte cupiditatum æstu animus fluctuat, quo pacto vitiis imperandum sit, non capit, sed post adeptam quietem prudens fit. Huc accedit, quod multa ex philosophia theoretica ad moralem philosophiam necessario petenda sunt, ut quid sit anima, quid affectus, quid proportio Arithmetica & Geometrica. Nec enim, ut Plato docet in Alcibiade, scire possumus, quomodo aliquid curandum sit, nisi eius naturam cognitamn atque exploratam prius habeamus. At moralis philosophia animæ & affectuum malorum mediatrix est. Præterea oportet moralem philosophum a naturali sumere, quæ sint animæ facultates, ut, quæ quibus subditæ esse debeant, & in quarum actione beatitudo posita sit, edoceat. Quapropter Aristoteles in formandâ morum scientiâ Physiologiam sæpe adhibet.We outline this order thus. Schools are either pedagogical or gymnastics. The former are intended for educating minors, the latter for those older and more advanced in age and judgment. In pedagogical schools, besides the rudiments of piety, the arts of speech should first be taught and learned: Lexicography, Grammar, and part of Rhetoric; then Logic, Oratory, and Poetics; finally, the beginnings of Arithmetic and Music. In gymnastics, which are called gymnasmata, a more comprehensive teaching of languages should be delivered, and thus the arts of speech and others impressed in pedagogical schools should be repeated here. Then the body of the whole philosophy should be presented to the strengthened minds of the listeners, as if through anatomy, so that pure Mathematics, that is, Arithmetic and Geometry, are taught first: then Physics are added, followed by Pneumatics and Metaphysics. Finally, practical philosophy should succeed as the conclusion of the philosophical course, from which a transition is made to some supreme capacity. Let us examine the reasoning behind each. Firstly, the arts of speech are the foremost of all in the order of better teaching: because disciplines nowadays are not taught in the vernacular language, as they once were. Here you will note that the part of Rhetoric concerning figures can be conveniently proposed before Logic, but not tropology: because the latter depends on the cognition of logical arguments. Secondly, pure Mathematics are prior to Physiology in the order of better teaching: because Mathematics aid the perception of other disciplines and there is less difficulty in learning them. Hence, due to the ease of learning, they have made the common name of disciplines (μαθήματα) their own. But Physics, since it investigates the hidden force of nature and largely depends on the vague and wandering knowledge of the senses, requires long observation and experience, and thus is much more difficult and labor-intensive. Therefore, the ancients taught their children mathematics, and Aristotle, in Ethics 6, chapter 8, states that a child can become a mathematician but not a wise man or a physicist. And Plato includes the mathematical disciplines among the musical, and barred those who were not trained in Mathematics, specifically Geometry, from his gymnasium, inscribing on the doors of the Academy: "Let no one ignorant of Geometry enter here." Thirdly, Metaphysics and Pneumatics are the last of all sciences in the order of better teaching. For the sciences that teach the most abstruse things and those removed from the habit of the senses should be learned last due to our frailty. This is indeed the case with Metaphysics and Pneumatics. In addition, the knowledge of what is treated in other sciences prepares and sharpens the mind for first philosophy, as Augustine explains in the second book of On Order, saying that human reason, when it aspires to know the higher things, should not attempt the ascent in vain or fall from a height, but should prepare itself with the inner disciplines as steps. Thus the philosopher gradually ascends through other sciences to Metaphysics and Pneumatics. Finally, practical philosophy is to be learned last in the order of better teaching. Indeed, a more mature judgment is needed for the understanding of how to cultivate morals, manage a family, and establish a state, than to grasp physical learning and any theoretical knowledge. And in those matters related to the discipline of life and the governance and moderation of human wills, experience is more deceptive and uncertain, and more arduous than in theoretical matters. This relates to Aristotle's statement in Ethics 1, chapter 3, that a young person is by no means suitable to be a listener of moral discipline; because, in the realm of perturbations, reliable judgment about actions cannot be firm, and, while the mind fluctuates with the tide of desires, it does not grasp how to command vices, but becomes wise after attaining calm. Additionally, many things must necessarily be sought from theoretical philosophy for moral philosophy, such as what the soul is, what affections are, what arithmetic and geometric proportion are. For we cannot know, as Plato teaches in Alcibiades, how something should be cared for unless we first have its nature known and explored. But moral philosophy is the healer of the soul and its evil affections. Moreover, the moral philosopher must take from natural philosophy what the faculties of the soul are, so that he may instruct which should be subject to which, and in the action of which happiness lies. Therefore, Aristotle often employs Physiology in forming the science of morals.
XXIII. Cursus Philosophiæ spatio trienni commodè absolvi potest, si fundamentum Philologiæ bene sit iactum in pædagogeo.XXIII. The course of Philosophy can be conveniently completed in a span of three years, if the foundation of Philology has been well laid in the school.
Nam primo sesquianno absolvi potest philosophia theoretica; altero, practica. Atque ita suprema aliqua facultas eligi potest, cui te totum consecres. Nam tres superiores facultates, Theologia, Iurisprudentia, & Medicina, doctrinæ ordine sunt posteriores ipsâ philosophiâ: quippe cum illæ utantur huius terminis & principiis. Si vero aliquis vitæ philosophicæ genus elegerit, ut facultatem istam docere velit, non suffecerit triennium, quâ de re in Didacticis.For the theoretical philosophy can be completed in the first year and a half; the practical, in the second. And thus, one can choose some supreme faculty to devote oneself entirely to. For the three higher faculties, Theology, Jurisprudence, and Medicine, are later than philosophy itself in the order of doctrine: since they utilize its terms and principles. But if someone has chosen a philosophical way of life, wishing to teach that faculty, three years will not be sufficient, as discussed in the Didactics.
XXIV. Inter tres superiores facultates duplex imprimis ordo attenditur.XXIV. Among the three higher faculties, two orders are primarily considered.
Prior ordo est dignitatis, quam istæ facultates obtinent ratione hominis, ut est homo, cuius perfectio pendet à bonis animæ, corporis, & fortunæ, ut vocant. Atque hâc ratione primas tenet Theologia, secundas Medicina, tertias Iurisprudentia. Posterior ordo est dignitatis, quam illæ ipsæ facultates obtinent ratione hominis, ut est pars societatis. Et cum Iurisprudentia primum locum obtinet; quia defendit Theologiam & Medicinam.The first order is of dignity, which these faculties obtain in regard to man as he is a man, whose perfection depends on the goods of the soul, body, and fortune, as they call it. In this regard, Theology holds the first place, Medicine the second, and Jurisprudence the third. The second order is of dignity, which these very faculties obtain in regard to man as he is part of society. And here Jurisprudence holds the first place, because it defends Theology and Medicine.
XXV. Inter artes mechanicas ordo naturæ, & dignitatis eodem modo constitui debet, ut inter artes liberales.XXV. Among the mechanical arts, the order of nature and dignity should be constituted in the same way as among the liberal arts.
Nam ordine naturæ priores sunt illæ artes mechanicæ, quæ sunt simpliciores, id est, quarum principia sunt simpliciora. Deinde illæ sunt digniores, quarum subiecta, & fines præstant. Quod attinet ad ordinem inventionis, ille colligi debet ex Chronologiâ.For in the order of nature, those mechanical arts are prior which are simpler, that is, whose principles are simpler. Then those are more dignified whose subjects and ends are more excellent. Regarding the order of discovery, it should be inferred from Chronology.
XXVI. Inter farragines disciplinarum ordo pendet a duobus principiis.XXVI. Among the conglomerates of disciplines, the order depends on two principles.
Primum est eiusmodi: Farragines disciplinarum sunt priores, quæ sunt propiores disciplinis homogeneis. Nam & illæ sunt disciplinæ propriæ dictæ. Quo quidem nomine censentur istæ quatuor: Mnemonica, Historica, Chronologia, & Architectonica. Secundum principium est: Farragines disciplinarum, quæ non sunt insignite nomine disciplinarum propriè dictarum, priores censentur, a quibus aliarum notitia pendet. Ita si conferas inter se Alchymiam, & Kabbalamiâ, illa pendet ab hâc.The first is of this kind: The conglomerates of disciplines are prior which are closer to homogeneous disciplines. For they are also the disciplines properly called. By this name, these four are considered: Mnemonics, History, Chronology, and Architecture. The second principle is: The conglomerates of disciplines that are not notably named disciplines properly speaking are considered prior, from which the knowledge of others depends. Thus, if you compare Alchemy and Kabbalah, the former depends on the latter.
PERORATIO TECHNOLOGIÆ.CONCLUSION OF TECHNOLOGY.
Talis est Technologia, secunda pars alphabeti Cyclopædæutici; quæ nobis exhibet latifundij nostri provincias, earumque cognitionem, & distinctionem. Quam nisi quis animo præconceperit suo, fieri non potest, ut cum affectu vel profectu versetur in disciplinarum studio. Quomodo enim, amabo, disciplinam amabis & admirabere, cuius sedem in regno hoc ignoras? Quomodo insuper cum fructu discas illam disciplinam, cuius affectiones, convenientiam, & differentiam non tenes?Such is Technology, the second part of the Cyclopaedic alphabet; which presents to us the provinces of our estate, and their knowledge and distinction. Unless one has preconceived this in one's mind, it is impossible to engage in the study of disciplines with affection or progress. For how, I ask, will you love and admire a discipline if you do not know its place in this realm? How, moreover, will you learn that discipline fruitfully if you do not grasp its affections, agreements, and differences?
